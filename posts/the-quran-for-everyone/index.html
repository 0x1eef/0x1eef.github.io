Introduction

This is a guest post by Farooq Karimi Zadeh. It was originally published on his Farsi blog.

In our Quran study sessions, we believe that everyone should be able to study the Quran and understand it through their own effort and reflection. The Quran must remain an accessible text. If its contents were encrypted and the key to unlocking it belonged only to a specific person or group, it would open the door to corruption. Anyone with the ability to think should be able to study and understand it.

Organized Religion

Lessons from Christianity

Personally, I argue that if the Quran is meant as the message and word of God for everyone, yet the key to understanding it resides only with certain people, then the door is open to corruption–just as it was for the Christian Bible. The Catholic Church claimed that only through them could one understand the Bible, and only through the Church could people be guided and receive God’s message.

This corruption eventually grew to the point where some corrupt priests were literally selling parts of paradise for money. At the same time, the Church neither allowed freedom of thought nor welcomed radically new ideas. For example, even though the Bible had no opinion on the shape of the Earth, the Church declared the Earth was flat and that the Sun and Moon orbited around it. When some scientists proposed the Earth’s roundness, they were persecuted, and some were even executed.

Your faith had to be approved by the Church, which determined who was a believer and who was an unbeliever.

Independent Religion

The Islamic Perspective

In contrast, Islam has never had a single central authority for laws or beliefs. As a result, some believe that “orthodox” and “heterodox” in Islam are meaningless, or that there is no such thing as “Orthodox Islam”. Others view Sunni Islam as orthodox. Even so, freedom of thought has not truly existed widespread among Muslims. In almost every century, there have been Muslims whose ideas diverged from the mainstream, and these people were often not treated well. In some cases, Muslims with “abnormal” thoughts were even executed.

In one of our Quranic study sessions, a participant objected, “Do you think that all the findings and opinions of scholars over the past 1,400 years are wrong, and your opinion is correct?” There was another participant whose views differed from the majority of Muslims. He replied, “These opinions were not written over the past 1,400 years–they were written only in 70 years.”

I didn’t understand this at first. But when I researched the four schools of thought in Sunni Islam, I realized something important. Sunni Islam can be considered synonymous with orthodox Islam: most Muslims are Sunni and follow one of these four schools. The founders of the four schools were Imam Abu Hanifa, Malik, Shafi’i, and Hanbal. The interesting thing is that these four religious leaders lived relatively close to each other in time:

  • Abu Hanifa: 80–150 AH
  • Malik: 93–179 AH
  • Shafi’i: 150–204 AH
  • Hanbal: 164–241 AH

The time between Imam Abu Hanifa’s birth and Imam Hanbal’s death is less than 200 years. Imam Malik was the teacher of Imam Shafi’i, who in turn was the teacher of Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal. Later scholars mostly followed one of these four schools. Of course, other schools of thought existed, but most have become extinct; even the Hanbali school today has very few followers. The two other still-existing schools are the Ja'fari and the Zahiri schools. Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq, considered the founder of the Ja'fari school, lived at the same time as Abu Hanifa. However, Ja'fari is considered a school in Shia Islam, not Sunni.

As a result, the Islam practiced today is not the product of 1,400 years of independent and free thought among scholars. Rather, it mostly stems from the tree planted in the early centuries of Islam. I don’t believe all the opinions of these schools are wrong, but I do think not everything we have today is fully aligned with the “Straight Path”.

Edited 5 September 2025: A friend took this post badly. He said:

“Islam is a revelation and was given by God. No one invented it. There is a difference between Islam and Fiqh. Fiqh is the invention and product of people.”

This is true–I believe the same. But for daily life, for example, for people in my city, the Fiqh of Imam Shafi’i is the only “Islam” they know. If you ask whether a girl with painted nails can perform a proper ablution (wudhu), the answer is that her wudhu is invalid because Imam Shafi’i said water should reach every part of the hand–but water cannot reach the natural nails. Does the Quran say water must reach everywhere? Or does it just tell us to wash our hands?

Or that you should recite Surah al-Hamd in every rak’ah of the prayer (salat) because Imam Shafi’i said so. Few people refer to the Quran or hadith to check what should be done in prayer. This becomes worse when sayings (in Arabic: zikr, plural: azkar) during bowing (ruku’) and prostration (sujud) are required because a mosque’s imam taught so–even though in Shafi’i fiqh, these sayings are not obligatory.
