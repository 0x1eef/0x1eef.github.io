About

I have been a Ruby programmer for more than ten years, and my biggest open source contribution was probably the time I spent on Pry’s core team. Since then I have started projects like llm.rb and an ecosystem of related projects, but I have decided to leave it behind and focus my efforts on golang instead.

I have no regrets or ill will towards anyone in the Ruby community, but I’ve seen the community changing and turning and I know that things just won’t be the same – a bit like a summer in Dublin 😉

Context

My perspective

Politics has entered the Ruby community in ways that are hard to ignore or brush aside and this has been done by leaders in the community whose words travel further and carry more weight than others in the community.

The breaking point for me was when I saw a leader within the Ruby community embrace and normalize Britain’s far-right – in particular, Tommy Robinson – a far-right extremist based in the UK, and a person who is no stranger to controversy.

I was already familiar with the far-right in the UK as an Irish person who grew up during “The Troubles”, and I know how dangerous it can be. It has a disturbing history that includes Combat 18’s riots in Lansdowne Road and it has been associated with loyalist terrorist groups in Northern Ireland.

Parallels

It is not difficult to see the parallels between Britain’s far-right and the loyalist groups in Northern Ireland. Both are extremists, ultra-nationalists who carry the same flags, repeat the same slogans, and spread the same bigotry.

Tommy Robinson and his movement are the modern face of the British far-right. Their targets have shifted over time, from Irish Catholics to Muslims and to anyone else considered not a “native Brit”, yet the underlying ideology has not changed. It is still based on hatred, ultra-nationalism, and defining an “other” who is the enemy.

Loyalism followed a similar path. It once defined itself in opposition to Irish Catholics, using violence and intimidation to enforce its ideology. In recent years, many loyalist voices have adopted the same Islamophobia and anti-migrant rhetoric that drives Robinson’s movement. The conspiracy theories have shifted: first a supposed Catholic takeover, now a supposed Muslim takeover.

The irony is that loyalists themselves were the product of migration during the plantation of Ulster. Yet today they direct their anger toward migrants, echoing the same fear and hatred spread by Britain’s far-right. The two movements have more in common than they have differences. That is why it alarms me to see figures within Ruby leadership normalize the far-right, and by extension loyalism. For me, that is a step too far, and it is why I am saying farewell to Ruby.

The Future

The open source community is diverse, and we can choose to be in communities that don’t carry the same baggage that Ruby does. I encourage anyone who feels uncomfortable in the Ruby community to find a different community that is more welcoming, and not to feel stuck in Ruby because they feel they have no other options.

Go

I decided that I will use golang going forward. I think it aligns particularly well with the kind of software I have been trying to create in Ruby, but with a language where the shoe is a better fit. Its package management system happens to be distributed, which is a big plus in my book, and I think that helps avoid situations we see at the moment with RubyCentral.

Although Go has some big personalities behind it, I have not seen the same kind of “cults of personality” that I have seen in the Ruby community. The community seems to be more focused on the language and its ecosystem, and governed by a committee, rather than a “benevolent dictator for life”. I think that is a healthier and more sustainable way to run a community, and I hope it continues that way.

The Plan

Moving from Ruby to Go is not going to be an overnight process, but I have already started. I have a few projects I want to do in Go, and I am increasing my knowledge of the language and its ecosystem. I am also looking for ways to contribute to the Go community.

At least in the short term, I will continue to maintain my Ruby projects, but they’re not going to be my main focus anymore. And eventually, I will stop maintaining them altogether.

Conclusion

Farewell Ruby

I am excited about the future, and I hope to see you in the Go community. As I said before, I have no ill will towards anyone in the Ruby community, but I think it is time for me to move on. I wish you all the best.
